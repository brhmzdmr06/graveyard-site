MoviePass: What Happens When Your Business Model Is the Bug

Founded: 2011Shut down: 2019Raised: $68MPeak users: 3M
The one-line verdict
MoviePass paid full price for cinema tickets and charged customers less than half that. They called it a strategy. It was just arithmetic they refused to do.

The Idea

MoviePass offered a simple proposition: pay $9.95 per month and see one film per day at any cinema in America. For regular cinemagoers it was an extraordinary deal. A single ticket costs $15 or more. Two films a month and you were already ahead. Within a year of dropping the price in 2017, MoviePass grew from 20,000 to 3 million subscribers.

The Math That Never Worked

MoviePass bought tickets at full retail price and charged a flat monthly fee far below cost. No volume discount, no revenue share with cinemas. If a subscriber saw just one film per month, MoviePass lost money. If they saw two, MoviePass lost more. At peak they were burning $40 million per month.

Lesson 1
If your unit economics are negative, you cannot grow your way to profitability. Build a model where each additional customer makes the business more sustainable, not less.

The Data Bet That Never Paid Off

MoviePass leadership knew the numbers did not work. Their bet was that user data would be valuable enough to subsidise losses. They would sell insights to studios and advertisers. None of it materialised at the scale needed. The data strategy was a hope, not a plan, and they were burning $40 million a month waiting for it.

Lesson 2
A future revenue hypothesis is not a business model. If your current model requires a speculative second act to become viable, stress-test that second act before committing to the first one.

The End

By 2019 MoviePass had restricted usage so heavily that the value proposition had evaporated. Subscribers left. The company shut down in September 2019. AMC and Regal then launched their own cinema subscription programmes with economics that actually worked.

Lesson 3
Your competitors will copy what works, not what you hoped would work. The cinema subscription idea was fine. MoviePass's execution was fatal. Someone else took the concept, fixed the unit economics, and survived.
