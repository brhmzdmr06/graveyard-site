The WeWork Collapse: What $47 Billion Taught Us About Unit Economics

Founded: 2010Bankruptcy: 2023Peak valuation: $47BTotal raised: $22B+
The one-line verdict
WeWork signed long-term leases and rented short-term. The math was broken from day one and $22 billion just made the hole bigger.

The Idea

WeWork leased office space long-term, divided it into flexible co-working spaces, and rented short-term at a premium. Give freelancers and startups the feel of a cool office without the traditional lease commitment. By 2019 WeWork was in 111 cities across 29 countries. The valuation hit $47 billion.

The Fatal Flaw

WeWork committed to 10 to 15 year leases and sold short-term memberships customers could cancel with minimal notice. In a downturn, customers cancel. WeWork still owed the rent. The model required perpetual growth to function.

At peak, WeWork was losing $219,000 per member per year. The more customers they added, the more money they lost.

Lesson 1
Know your unit economics before you scale. If you lose money on every customer, growth accelerates the collapse. Make sure your contribution margin is positive before investing in growth.

The Tech Company Illusion

WeWork's valuation only made sense if you believed it was a technology company. Neumann pitched community and the future of work. Investors applied Silicon Valley multiples to a real estate business. When WeWork filed its IPO in 2019, analysts saw the losses. The valuation collapsed from $47 billion to under $10 billion in weeks. The IPO was pulled. Neumann was ousted.

Lesson 2
Your valuation is not your business model. Build a business that survives due diligence, not one that depends on investors never looking too closely.

Covid Finished the Job

Office occupancy collapsed. Members cancelled. The lease obligations remained. WeWork filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy in November 2023, thirteen years after founding. Most of the $22 billion raised was gone.

Lesson 3
Always model the downside. WeWork's model worked in growth but nobody stress-tested a 30% occupancy drop. If the answer to that question is bankruptcy, fix the model before you scale.
